How to use

The Positive Sustainability Design Toolkit is here to support you as you design everyday experiences that help people feel good and do good. Whether you're creating a new product, refining a service, or simply exploring opportunities for positive impact, this toolkit provides research-grounded insights that can inspire meaningful, sustainable design.

Who can benefit

  • Product and service designers
  • UX/UI designers and researchers
  • Sustainability practitioners and circular economy innovators
  • Design educators and students

Each of the 49 positive practices is presented in a way that supports hands-on design work.

Think of these elements as small windows: each offers a different angle on what makes the practice feel good, work well, and naturally support sustainable behavior.

This toolkit offers multiple pathways to discover practices that align well-being with sustainability. Whether you're beginning a new project, designing for a specific context, or working toward particular emotional or environmental goals, you can navigate the 49 practices through four complementary approaches.

Exploration Mode

Use the hero sentence builder to filter practices interactively. Select everyday activities, "feel good" qualities, and "do good" behaviors to discover relevant practices.

  • Try this when: You're seeking general inspiration or starting a new project
  • Example: Filter for "cooking" + "meaningful" + "pro-ecological" to discover practices around transforming meal prep into creative rituals that minimize food waste.

Context-Based Discovery

Choose a specific home activity domain (cooking, cleaning, etc.) and explore all practices within that context.

  • Try this when: You're designing for a particular product category or use context
  • Example: Exploring all "clothing care" practices reveal patterns around mindful consumption, community sharing, and creative care routines that could inform a laundry product redesign.

Well-Being Lens

Filter by the type of well-being you want to support using enjoyable tags to explore hedonic well-being, meaningful tags for eudaimonic well-being, or both!

  • Try this when: You have specific emotional or psychological goals for your design
  • Example: Focusing on "both hedonia and eudaimonia" reveals practices that offer immediate sensory pleasure while building meaningful skills or relationships.

Sustainability Lens

Select a sustainability behavior type you want to promote (frugal, pro-ecological, altruistic, or equitable). Explore practices that naturally encourage this behavior.

  • Try this when: Your project has specific environmental or social impact goals
  • Example: Exploring "altruistic" practices might reveal opportunities for neighborhood sharing systems or community-oriented features in home products.

You can adapt the toolkit's use to your working style and project structure. Use it independently to guide your design process from discovery through evaluation, or leverage it as a collaborative framework to align teams, engage stakeholders, and co-create with users.

Individual Design Work

STEP 1: Discover and Empathize

Identify which practices resonate with your target users and where gaps exist between current behaviors and desired experiences. Map key moments before, during, and after product interactions, selecting practices that match emotions and behaviors at each touchpoint.

STEP 2: Find Inspiration and Ideate

Use any navigation approach to discover 3–5 relevant practices. Brainstorm how to translate these patterns into your design context. Generate variations and combine elements from multiple practices for novel solutions.

STEP 3: Prototype and Enrich

Design interventions that facilitate specific practices, testing whether they trigger intended emotions and behaviors. Revisit existing designs through the lens of positive practices, layering in savoring techniques or sustainability behaviors you hadn't considered.

STEP 4: Evaluate

Assess concepts against dual-impact criteria. Measure both emotional resonance and sustainability outcomes.

Collaborative Sessions

STEP 1: Discover and Co-create

Explore practices with users, clients, or domain experts as conversation starters. Understand which practices resonate and identify gaps between current and desired experiences.

STEP 2: Ideate Together

In small groups, select 2–5 practices and brainstorm integration possibilities. Consider the main product context, and positive emotions, well-being, or sustainability domains aimed to be achieved.

STEP 3: Prototype and Test

Sketch and prototype design interventions that facilitate specific practices, based on the design toolkit. Collaboratively test whether they trigger the intended emotions and behaviors.

STEP 4: Evaluate collectively

Assess and iterate concept developments through the dual-impact lens. Assess feasibility and desirability together, measuring both emotional resonance and sustainability outcomes. Refine based on collective insight.

These practices offer starting points, not prescriptions. To create truly impactful designs, adapt patterns to your specific context, validate them with your users, and explicitly connect the emotional and sustainability dimensions. The following considerations will help you translate research insights into design solutions that resonate with your audience and amplify dual impact.

Adapt to Your Context

All the practices are home contextual patterns and inspirations. Translate them creatively to fit your product/service types.

You may think about these questions for your design:

  • How can I apply this practice pattern to fit my specific product context and user needs?
  • How do the physical materials, positive emotions, and sustainable behavioral elements of this practice work together in my design?

Test with Your Users

These practices emerged from research with young adults (18–25) in the United States using home appliances. Validate how patterns manifest for your specific users across different ages, cultures, and product categories.

These questions may strengthen understanding of users:

  • How can I translate this practice pattern to fit my target users' age, cultural background, and living situations?
  • How can I validate these patterns with my specific audience?

Connect the Dots

Connect emotional benefits explicitly to sustainability outcomes rather than treating them separately. Help people see the dual benefit. Design cues, feedback, or celebrations that highlight these experiences.

These questions may help your design development:

  • Does my design clearly link the positive feelings users experience with the sustainable behaviors they're performing?
  • How might my design make this positive practice more noticeable and accessible in users' homes?

About

Positive Sustainability Design Toolkit:

Discover practices that feel good and do good

The Positive Sustainability Design Toolkit is an open-access online resource created to help you design everyday experiences that uplift people's well-being while supporting sustainable behaviors. We call these positive practices, which are interaction patterns with everyday technologies that help people feel good and act in ways that do good.

Through in-depth research with young adults (18–25 years old) across the United States, we identified 20 distinct positive practices observed in how people engage with home appliances. These practices appear across five everyday contexts of food, cleaning, clothing care, air care, and personal care, and show how simple routines can support both personal fulfillment and responsible living.

Think of your daily routine. What moments bring you joy?

Perhaps experimenting with a new recipe, the satisfaction of a clean space, or the pride in caring for a cherished item. These aren't just mundane tasks. These everyday interactions are rich with potential for both personal fulfillment and environmental responsibility. Everything you create shapes how people feel and act in their daily lives.

The toolkit aims to inspire you with rich information on the convergence of well-being and sustainability, and to encourage you to explore these synergies in your own design work. It can be used by anyone interested in creating positive everyday experiences: a product designer, a UX researcher, a sustainability practitioner, or anyone else working to shape how people live at home.

Understanding Well-being

In this toolkit, "feel good" represents well-being. We distinguish between two types of well-being experiences:

  • Hedonic well-being focuses on pleasure, enjoyment, and positive sensory experiences. Practices labeled as "enjoyable" support this dimension.
  • Eudaimonic well-being focuses on meaning, purpose, and personal growth. Practices labeled as "meaningful" support this dimension.
  • Practices marked as both "enjoyable and meaningful" support both types of well-being simultaneously.

Our research draws on 10 savoring strategies (Bryant & Veroff, 2017), which are psychological techniques that help people consciously attend to and prolong positive experiences. Savoring has been demonstrated to enhance happiness, resilience, and adaptive coping in psychology research, yet its application in sustainable technology design remains underexplored.

Understanding Sustainability

In this toolkit, "do good" represents sustainability. We distinguish between four types of sustainable behaviors:

  • Frugal behaviors focus on mindful resource use and waste reduction.
  • Pro-ecological behaviors focus on protecting natural environments.
  • Altruistic behaviors focus on benefiting others and your community.
  • Equitable behaviors focus on fair distribution of resources and opportunities.

These four behavior domains (Corral-Verdugo et al., 2011; 2012) are interconnected. A single practice may also support multiple types of sustainable behavior.

This toolkit is an initiative of Meta Design Technology Lab at Cornell University. The research investigates how everyday technologies can be systematically designed to foster sustainability and well-being simultaneously through theories of savoring and sustainable behaviors. We believe that designing for dual impact in well-being and sustainability is not just possible but essential. Thank you for being part of this journey toward more positive, sustainable futures.

FAQ

This toolkit reflects patterns observed among young adults (18–25 years old) in the United States, focusing on interactions with home appliances. We chose this demographic as our starting point to deeply understand how emerging adults, who are forming lifelong habits, engage with everyday technologies in ways that support both well-being and sustainability.

However, we recognize that practice preferences and engagement patterns evolve throughout life, and that cultural contexts significantly shape how practices are experienced and valued.

We encourage designers to use these practices as inspiration and starting points, validating and adapting them for their specific users and contexts. Your insights from different age groups, cultural backgrounds, or living situations can help future iterations better serve the design community.

While our research focused on home appliances across five contexts of food, cleaning, clothing care, air care, and personal care, the underlying patterns of positive practices can inspire innovations across diverse everyday services and products.

The principles of how people savor experiences while engaging in sustainable behaviors are applicable to many design challenges. We encourage you to explore how these patterns might translate to your specific domain.
